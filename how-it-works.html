How Samaritam Works

Truth told
in balance.

Samaritam analyses any text — news articles, tweets, speeches, opinion pieces — for political bias, emotional manipulation, framing patterns, and factual integrity. Grounded in peer-reviewed academic research, not opinion.

Most bias-checking tools rate entire news outlets. Samaritam rates the specific piece in front of you — because even a generally centrist outlet can publish a biased op-ed. The difference matters.

The Name

Samaritam is a compound of two Sanskrit roots, chosen to reflect exactly what the tool does.

Sama
Sanskrit
Balance · Equanimity · Equal-mindedness
Ritam
Sanskrit · Vedic
Cosmic truth · The natural order of things · What is real

Together: balanced truth — or truth in its natural, undistorted form. That is what Samaritam attempts to surface in every piece it analyses.

Seven lenses. One clear picture.

Every analysis breaks down the text across seven dimensions, grouped into two sections.

Section 1 — Spectrum Scores

Political Lean

Where does the text sit on the political spectrum? From far-left to far-right, mapped using Moral Foundations Theory.

Economic Axis

Does it favour state intervention or free-market approaches? Economic framing is often where political lean hides most effectively.

Social Axis

Progressive or traditional? Where the text sits on social values, independent of its economic stance.

Rhetorical Temperature

Is the language designed to inform or to provoke? Charged words are flagged inline so you can see exactly where emotion replaces evidence.

Section 2 — Deep Reads

Emotional Language

Specific charged words are flagged with an explanation of why each one is emotionally loaded rather than neutral.

Framing Bias

Who gets quoted? Whose perspective dominates? Whose voice is absent? Framing is the invisible architecture of every piece of writing.

Factual Density

What ratio of the text is verifiable claims versus opinion or inference? A single number that says a lot about the writer's intent.

Headline vs Body

Does the headline fairly represent what the article actually says? Sensationalist headlines on measured reporting are one of the most common forms of bias.

The Academic Framework

Samaritam's analysis is anchored in three bodies of peer-reviewed research. The AI is not giving its personal opinion — it is applying frameworks validated by independent researchers.

01

Moral Foundations Theory — Jonathan Haidt, NYU

Six moral foundations — care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty — that underpin political thinking across cultures. Left-leaning thought tends to weight care and fairness heavily; right-leaning thought distributes weight more evenly. Cross-cultural, peer-reviewed.

02

Political Compass Model

Two independent axes — economic (state intervention vs free market) and social (progressive vs traditional). Prevents the reductive mistake of collapsing all political views onto a single left-right line.

03

NLP Political Bias Research — Stanford & MIT

Published research identifying specific linguistic markers — word choice, sentence framing, rhetorical patterns — that correlate with political lean in text. Applied to flag emotional language and framing patterns.

A note on bias in the tool itself

Any tool that analyses political bias has to reckon with its own potential bias. We've addressed this by anchoring every analysis to established academic frameworks rather than the AI's own tendencies. The AI is explicitly instructed to apply Haidt's Moral Foundations definitions and Political Compass axes — not to form its own judgement.

No system is perfect. If you notice systematic skewing in Samaritam's results, we'd genuinely like to know. Reach out via the Contact Us page.

My Sources — the outlet tracker

Every time you analyse an article and save the outlet, Samaritam adds it to your personal Sources tracker. Over time this builds a picture of each outlet's typical bias profile based on your own analyses.

The scores shown in My Sources are global averages — calculated across all Samaritam users who have analysed articles from that outlet. The more people analyse, the more reliable the average becomes.

The Top 10 Global leaderboard ranks outlets by rhetorical neutrality — the sources that inform without provoking, presenting facts over emotion. It appears once an outlet has been analysed at least 10 times across the community.

How your data is stored

If you sign in with Google, your personal outlet list is backed up to our secure database and accessible from any device. Scores contribute to the global averages.

If you don't sign in, your outlet list is saved locally in your browser. It works fully but won't survive if you clear your browser data. Your analyses still contribute to global averages.
